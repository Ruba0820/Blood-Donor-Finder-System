Why Donate?

  • Donating blood may reduce the risk of heart disease and stimulate red blood cell generation.
  • It lowers the amount of stored toxins circulating in your bloodstream.
  • The good news — you can give blood again and again to save more lives.
  • Regular donors can safely give blood once every 12 weeks.

World Blood Donor Day

Every year on 14th June, the world observes World Blood Donor Day — a date chosen in honour of Karl Landsteiner, who discovered the ABO blood group system. It's a day to thank voluntary donors and encourage more people to start donating.

Blood Donor Eligibility

You should not be suffering from any of the following, or currently under medication for them:

Hepatitis B, C
AIDS
Diabetes (on medication)
Fits / Convulsions (on medication)
Cancer
Leprosy or other infectious diseases
Severe allergies
Hemophilia / Bleeding problems
Kidney disease
Heart disease
Hormonal disorders
Jaundice (within 5 years)
Tuberculosis (within 2 years)
Chicken Pox (within 1 year)
Malaria (within 1 year)
Organ Transplant (within 1 year)
Blood Transfusion (within 6 months)
Pregnancy (within 6 months)

Blood Facts in General

  • There is no substitute for human blood.
  • Blood makes up about 7% of your body's weight.
  • An average adult has about 14 to 18 pints of blood.
  • One standard unit or pint of blood equals about two cups.
  • Blood carries oxygen and nutrients to every part of the body.
  • Blood carries carbon dioxide and waste products to the lungs, kidneys, and liver for disposal.
  • Blood fights infection and helps heal wounds.
  • One unit of donated whole blood is separated into components — red cells, white cells, plasma, and platelets.
  • There are four main blood types: A, B, AB, and O.
  • Each blood type is either Rh positive or Rh negative.
